1. Introduction

What this document covers and the recommended two-layer fraud policy.

This report evaluates card- and ACH-based fraud signals for ABC business accounts. The dominant pattern in the sample is a short-lived, card-funded bust-out: accounts open, spend heavily on high-risk MCCs (gift cards, apparel, quasi-cash), and cash out within days — not slow ACH draining.

Recommended production policy — two layers combined with OR logic:

  • Tier 1 — Immediate — velocity and bust-out rules at account start. Fully automated holds and limits; no human review at scale.
  • Tier 2 — Confirm — high-precision corroborating evidence (specific MCCs, vendor scores, returned ACH). Mostly auto-freeze; manual review only for ambiguous edge cases.

Eleven atomic rules roll out in a fixed order (5 Immediate, then 6 Confirm). The sections below describe how the analysis was built, the underlying data, walk through the policy and how each step adds coverage, the fraud economics, the full rule catalog, and worked case-study examples of how the fraud was actually committed.

2. Approach

How this analysis was built — from exploratory notebook work through hypothesis-driven probing to the production rule library in this report.

Phase 1 — Exploratory analysis in Jupyter (ABC_Risk.ipynb)

Started in a Jupyter notebook: load data/businesses.csv and data/transactions.csv, merge labels, and run structured EDA before any policy decisions.

  • Data quality — checked duplicates, micro-deposit ACH patterns (amounts < $1), and the 5 labeled businesses with no transaction history.
  • Bad-rate by category — for every categorical field (entry type, decline reason, MCC, country, AVS, contactless, etc.), computed business-level bad rate and lift vs. the 84% baseline.
  • Business feature flat file — aggregated to one row per business with lifetime count/ever flags → business_features_lifetime.csv (323 × 206 columns).
  • MCC deep-dive — ranked merchant categories by business-level bad rate (businesses with >1 txn at that MCC). Men's/women's clothing (~94%), women's accessory (~97%), and postal (~83%) rose to the top.
  • Feature ranking (IV / WOE) — calc_iv() scored every engineered feature by Information Value; features with IV ≥ 0.02 kept for modelling (transaction-level, then business-level after aggregation).
  • Decision tree (interpretable model) — DecisionTreeClassifier (max depth 4) on business-level lifetime / 7-day / 14-day features. Test AUC 0.89. Top split: contactless spend in the first 7 days.
  • First rules — hand-built rules in the notebook (cells 58–59), exported to rule_effectiveness.csv and summarized in Risk_Brief.md: Rule 2 (span ≤ 7d + decline ≥ 5%) or Rule 4 (span ≤ 14d + ACH-out ≥ $1k in first 7d) → 96.5% precision, 60.3% recall, 6 FP. Rule 3 (CNP + AVS fail) was tested but rejected (51% precision). Good starting point, but missed card-heavy bust-outs.

Phase 2 — Hypothesis-driven probing

Notebook patterns raised specific questions. Each was investigated on outgoing spend only (direction = from) with a severity lens — bad rate × average charge size — then turned into testable atomic rules.

Key questions that shaped the rules

  • USPS / postal MCC — Is the signal in-person ~$3k PO counter swipes (AVS fail, contactless) vs. legit online postage? Can fraudsters cash out via Western Union / MoneyGram (MCC 4829) instead of USPS?
  • Apparel / Lululemon — Is the bust-out vendor-specific or generalizable to ≥3 clothing MCC swipes ≥$250?
  • Card bust-out velocity — Do bad accounts spike on day 0? Gift-card-style amounts ($200–$1,000)? Bursts of contactless + AVS FAIL?
  • Money movement — Is this ACH draining or card-funded bust-out? How material are returned ACH-ins (issuer clawback)?
  • Quasi-cash / money orders — Should policy target USPS only or all quasi-cash MCC codes (4829, 6051, 9402)?
  • International — Non-US merchant declines and settled foreign txns (e.g. Poczta Polska postal)?
  • Micro-deposits — Useful as a fraud rule or only operational account-verification gating?
  • Production shape — How to layer Immediate (velocity) vs. Confirm (high-precision MCC evidence) for automation at scale?

Phase 3 — Rule library & production policy

Findings were codified in fraud_complete_analysis.py: 37 atomic rules (R01–R37), each evaluated for precision, recall, false positives, and bad-dollar capture. Rules were ranked and stacked with OR logic; a curated 11-rule production rollout (5 Immediate + 6 Confirm) was chosen for automation — not a greedy max-recall stack. This report (fraud_analysis.html) summarizes the production policy and supporting analysis.

  • What changed from the notebook — shifted from ACH-velocity-only (Risk_Brief) to card/MCC signals: apparel, quasi-cash, GC-style swipes, contactless+AVS, international, returned ACH.
  • What stayed — business-level aggregation, explicit precision/recall trade-offs, interpretable boolean rules (not a black-box score) for ops teams.

Labeling & scope. Each business carries a binary is_bad flag (fraud confirmed vs. good). Money-movement conventions. For ACH, direction = to is money into the account (funding) and from is money out; a RETURNED ACH-in is an issuer clawback (a realized loss). Card transactions are direction = from (spend). These conventions drive every rule.

6. Fraud pattern & money flows

Where fraud dollars move in the sample — and which merchant categories concentrate the spend that rules target.

Bad business money flows

What this shows: total dollars summed across all 276 confirmed-fraud businesses with transactions (Jun–Dec 2022), in $ thousands. Each bar is a separate category — not a balanced in/out diagram for one account.

  • ACH IN ($129k) — external funding into accounts. Small aggregate: only ~38% of bad businesses had any ACH-in at all.
  • ACH OUT ($1.2M) — transfers out to external banks. Large, but often after card activity, not the primary bust-out vector.
  • Card spend ($933k) — settled card swipes (apparel, quasi-cash, GC-style amounts). This is where most rules fire.
  • Returned ACH ($322k) — inbound ACH that clawed back (issuer loss). Motivates auto-freeze rule R11.

Why it’s relevant: confirms the case-study pattern — short-lived bust-out via card at high-risk MCCs, not slow ACH draining. Median bad account has ~$0 ACH-in but ~$3k card spend and ~$4k ACH-out. The chart justifies card/MCC rules (R03, R06, R21, R24) alongside ACH-out velocity rules.

Top fraud MCCs

What this shows: merchant categories ranked by total bad outbound dollars (outgoing card spend only). Bars are $ thousands across all bad businesses; chart shows the top 8 categories.

  • Clothing stores ($647k, 94% bad rate) — dominant fraud dollar sink. Repeat high-ticket swipes at resellable apparel (Lululemon pattern) → R03.
  • Postal / government ($167k, 83% bad rate) — high average ticket (~$1,500). In-person PO counter swipes for money orders, not legit online postage → R06.
  • Women's accessory ($78k, 97% bad rate) — highest bad rate in the top tier; small volume but very concentrated fraud.
  • Drug stores ($57k, 73% bad rate) — gift-card-style amount patterns and prepaid velocity → R21, R25.
  • Grocery / convenience ($56k + $53k, ~52–60% bad rate) — meaningful volume but weaker signal than apparel or postal; included for context, not primary rule drivers.

Takeaway: fraud concentrates in resellable goods (clothing) and quasi-cash exits (postal MO, wire/MO MCCs). High-dollar categories with bad rates below ~80% are poor standalone rule candidates — rules target the top of this chart, not every MCC that appears.

Key metrics

Counts and rates used in tables and decision-tree nodes throughout this report.

TP — true positive
A confirmed fraud account that the rule correctly flags. Counted in precision and recall numerators when the business is bad and flagged.
FP — false positive
A good (legitimate) account wrongly flagged by the rule. Each FP is a false alarm — the cost of being aggressive. Shown as +FP when a new rule step adds more good accounts to the flagged set.
FN — false negative
A confirmed fraud account the rule misses (not flagged). Drives recall: more FN means lower recall.
Flagged
Total businesses caught by the rule or stack so far (TP + FP). “Cum. flagged” grows as OR-rules are added.
Cum. bad rate / Marg. bad rate
Cumulative bad rate = % bad among everyone flagged so far (TP ÷ flagged). Marginal bad rate = % bad among only the new flags from this step (+Bad ÷ (+Bad + +FP)).
Precision
% of flagged businesses that are actually bad (TP ÷ (TP + FP)). Higher = fewer false alarms.
Recall
% of all bad businesses in the sample that the rule catches (TP ÷ (TP + FN)). Higher = fewer frauds missed.
Bad-$ capture
% of total bad outbound dollars linked to businesses flagged by the rule.

Rule types & categories

atomic Atomic rule
Single, testable rule (R01–R37) with one clear trigger — e.g. “≥3 GC swipes same day.” Evaluated on its own for precision/recall.
tier Tier 1 — Immediate vs Tier 2 — Confirm
Immediate = velocity/bust-out rules → automated holds and limits (no human). Confirm = high-precision MCC/vendor evidence → mostly auto-freeze at scale; manual review optional only for lower-precision rules (e.g. R06 quasi-cash).
velocity Velocity / ACH
Rules based on money-movement speed or ACH patterns — short account span, ACH-out > ACH-in, returned ACH, F2F funding, high outbound volume.
apparel Apparel
Men's/Women's clothing or accessory MCC swipes — bust-out at apparel stores (generalized from Lululemon pattern).
gc contactless GC / Contactless
Gift-card-style amount patterns, contactless + AVS FAIL bursts, drug-store prepaid velocity.
day0 Day-0
First-day or first-few-days outbound burst — account opens and immediately spends or cashes out.
international International
Non-US merchant declines or settled foreign card transactions, especially high-risk countries.
high severity High severity
Quasi-cash MCC codes (4829 wire/MO, 6051 FX/MO, 9402 postal, plus watchlist codes below), Sardine VERY_HIGH score, or similar vendor/MCC signals with high fraud correlation.
precision Precision tier
Composite of 100%-precision atomic rules — intended for auto-action with minimal false positives.
composite Composite
Legacy multi-rule bundles (TIER_C, TIER_D, etc.) from earlier analysis iterations.

Quasi-cash / money-order MCC registry (R06)

Production rule matches primary MCC codes only (integer match, no vendor regex). Watchlist codes are documented for monitoring — not in R06 until validated.

Four confirmed-fraud accounts from the sample, showing exactly how the bust-out was executed and which rules would have flagged it. All figures are from the raw transaction ledger.

A. Lululemon apparel bust-out

business_2MxWacneKwt8 · 42 txns over 8 days (Oct 25 – Nov 2, 2022)

Settled card-out$26,999
Card swipes30
ACH funded in$50,001
Returned ACH-in$10,000
  1. Account funded via a large ACH-in ($50k), part of which later returned ($10k clawback = realized loss).
  2. Within an 8-day window, ran 22 clothing-store swipes concentrated at Lululemon (Freehold, King of Prussia, Legacy Place) — buying resellable apparel to launder funds.
  3. Repeat same-store, high-ticket purchases across multiple locations in days — a classic reseller bust-out, not normal retail.

Flagged by: R03 ≥3 clothing swipes ≥$250 · R11 returned ACH · R27/R28 early bust-out

B. Postal / money-order cash-out

business_MtAoUqdgXR57 · 71 txns over 44 days (Oct 19 – Dec 3, 2022)

Settled card-out$15,750
Money-order / wire12 txns
USPS postal7 txns
Returned ACH-in$8,000
  1. Converted account balance to quasi-cash: 12 money-order/wire-transfer swipes (incl. Cash App P2P coded 4829) and 7 USPS postal money-order purchases.
  2. Money orders and wires are effectively untraceable cash — the fastest clean exit once funds land.
  3. $8,000 of the funding ACH later returned, confirming the deposit was never good.

Flagged by: R06 quasi-cash MCC (4829/6051/9402) + outbound ≥$1.5k · R11 returned ACH

  3. Part of the $23.9k funding ACH returned, again a realized clawback.
